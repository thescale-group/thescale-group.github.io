// 01 · Biophysics & Epigenomics

Genome Organization

The Grand Challenge

The genome is not a static, linear string of letters; it is a highly dynamic, 2-meter-long 3D polymer operating inside a microscopic nucleus. How this molecule folds physically determines which genes are expressed and which remain silent. Understanding these structural mechanics is the key to unlocking the mysteries of cellular differentiation, evolutionary biology, and genetic diseases.

Our Computational Approach

We approach the nucleus through the lens of statistical polymer physics. By building advanced, physics-based simulations calibrated with experimental Hi-C contact maps, we recreate the folding architecture of chromosomes. We model the molecular motors (like CTCF and cohesin) that drive loop extrusion, allowing us to actively perturb the genome *in silico* and mathematically predict the resulting shifts in transcriptional output.

Recent Proofs of Concept
  • Predicting Transcription Directly from Structure We established a computational framework that quantitatively predicts gene expression levels directly from the 3D chromatin contact probability—bypassing the need for DNA sequence data entirely. [npj Syst. Biol. Appl., 2025]
  • Ribosome Dynamics & Information Flow Beyond the nucleus, we extend our modeling to translation, using Ribo-seq data to build kinetic models that capture ribosome stacking and rate-limiting steps in protein synthesis. [Front. Mol. Biosci., 2021]
// 02 · Sustainable Energy

Electrolytes & Interphases

The Grand Challenge

The global transition to a sustainable energy grid requires batteries that charge in minutes, hold massive amounts of energy, and will never catch fire. The primary bottleneck holding back this reality is the electrolyte and its highly reactive interface with the metal anode (the SEI). We must move beyond trial-and-error chemistry to rationally design ion-transport highways.

Our Computational Approach

We explore the entire spectrum of ion transport. For liquid systems, we utilize classical MD to understand how manipulating solvent chemistry (like fluorination) alters atomic solvation shells and suppresses dendrite growth. For the future of batteries, we train Graph Neural Networks on quantum-mechanical DFT data to rapidly screen thousands of novel solid-state electrolyte structures, discovering safe, hyper-conductive ceramics.

Recent Proofs of Concept
  • AI-Accelerated Solid-State Discovery We deployed machine learning to screen vast compositional spaces, identifying unprecedented solid-state sodium-ion conductors with ideal electrochemical stability windows. [PCCP, 2025]
  • Decoding Solvation Mechanics By atomistically simulating fluorinated ether-based solvents, we discovered structural diffusion mechanisms that fundamentally change how we suppress dendrite growth in room-temperature batteries. [J. Mater. Chem. A, 2025; ACS Appl. Energy Mater., 2025]
// 03 · Quantum Tunability

Quantum Electrode Materials

The Grand Challenge

Storing energy and processing information at the ultimate physical limit requires entirely new classes of materials. 2D materials (like MXenes) and complex intercalation lattices offer unprecedented tunability—but only if we can master their behavior at the quantum-mechanical level before heading to the synthesis lab.

Our Computational Approach

Acting as theoretical architects, we rely on First-Principles Density Functional Theory (DFT) to build materials atom by atom. We predict complex phase diagrams, calculate intercalation voltages, and map electron spin densities. This allows us to explicitly design surface terminations for enhanced supercapacitors, or engineer Janus interfaces for next-generation Spintronic logic devices.

Recent Proofs of Concept
  • Design Rules for MXene Stability We established comprehensive predictive maps detailing how coordination symmetry, transition metals, and surface terminations dictate the phase stability and electrochemical limits of MXenes. [J. Phys. Chem. C, 2023; J. Energy Storage, 2025]
  • Nanoscale Tribology & Spintronics We identified a novel "sub-Moiré" friction mechanism operating within twisted bilayer heterostructures, and designed highly efficient spin-injection contacts using Janus TMD interfaces. [J. Phys. Condens. Matter, 2025; ACS Nano, 2024]
