Battery Materials

We use first-principles DFT, classical and reactive molecular dynamics, and machine-learning interatomic potentials to understand and design the full spectrum of battery components ‚Äî from solid and liquid electrolytes to composite interfaces and electrode materials ‚Äî targeting next-generation Na-ion, Li-metal, and Mg-ion systems.

01.A ‚Äî‚Äî
Solid-State Electrolytes
We combine DFT and machine-learning approaches to screen, design, and characterise solid-state electrolytes for Na and Li systems. Our recent ML study (PCCP 2025) screened thousands of candidates using graph neural networks trained on DFT data, identifying high-conductivity structures with wide electrochemical stability windows. We also study fluorination effects on ether-based electrolytes, revealing structural diffusion mechanisms beyond the vehicular transport picture.

Liquid Electrolytes & Solvation
Classical MD simulations reveal how solvation structure of Na‚Å∫ and Li‚Å∫ in ether-based electrolytes dictates SEI composition, dendrite formation, and long-term cycling stability. We systematically study how fluorination of ether solvents tunes the solvation shell and suppresses dendrite growth ‚Äî enabling room-temperature Na‚ÄìS batteries with significantly improved reversibility.

01.C ‚Äî‚Äî
Composite Electrolytes & Interphases
The solid-electrolyte interphase (SEI) determines battery lifetime but remains poorly understood at the molecular level. Using reactive MD and DFT, we decode SEI formation mechanisms ‚Äî identifying decomposition pathways of ether and carbonate solvents and their role in building stable multiphasic interphases on Na and Li anodes. We also study MXene-based composite electrolyte interfaces for supercapacitor applications.

Electrode Materials
DFT calculations guide the design of cathode and anode materials for Na-ion and Mg-ion batteries. We have studied Prussian blue analogue cathodes (Al-doped NaMnHCF), LiFePO‚ÇÑ for fast-charging Li-ion batteries, Mg‚ÄìSn alloy anodes undergoing near-room-temperature phase transformation, and defective graphene-based anodes. Ab initio phase diagrams and voltage profiles directly inform experimental synthesis.

Genome Organization & Gene Regulation

Inside every cell nucleus, ~2 metres of DNA is folded into a compact 3D structure that directly controls which genes are active. We build polymer-physics models of chromatin architecture ‚Äî calibrated against Hi-C contact maps ‚Äî to decode how 3D genome folding drives gene expression and how structural perturbations propagate to transcriptional change.

02.A ‚Äî‚Äî
3D Chromatin Architecture
Chromosomes are not random polymers ‚Äî they adopt precise hierarchical structures: compartments (A/B), Topologically Associating Domains (TADs), and chromatin loops. We model these using bead-spring polymer simulations informed by experimental Hi-C data, capturing how architectural proteins like CTCF and cohesin organise the genome spatially and functionally.

02.B ‚Äî‚Äî
Gene Expression Prediction
Our 2025 npj paper demonstrated that gene expression levels can be quantitatively predicted directly from the 3D chromatin contact probability of a locus ‚Äî without knowing the underlying DNA sequence. This causal link between structure and transcription opens a new window into gene regulation in differentiation, disease, and development.
Gene Expression RNA-seq Contact Probability Transcription
02.C ‚Äî‚Äî
Chromosome‚ÄìTranscription Coupling
Altering chromosome structure ‚Äî through deletion of CTCF sites, cohesin mutations, or epigenetic reprogramming ‚Äî reshapes TAD boundaries and rewires gene expression programmes. We model how these perturbations propagate, providing a quantitative, physics-based framework for understanding enhancer‚Äìpromoter communication across genomic distances.
Enhancer‚ÄìPromoter Structural Perturbation Loop Extrusion Epigenetics
02.D ‚Äî‚Äî
Translation & Ribosome Dynamics
Beyond the nucleus, we extend our quantitative modelling to translation ‚Äî using ribosome profiling (Ribo-seq) data to build kinetic models of protein synthesis. These models capture ribosome stacking, codon usage effects, and rate-limiting steps, connecting genome-level information flow to protein production.
Ribo-seq Kinetic Modelling Protein Synthesis Codon Usage

MXenes, 2D Spintronics & vdW Heterostructures

Two-dimensional materials offer extraordinary tunability of electronic, magnetic, and mechanical properties. We use DFT and MD to design nitride MXenes for spintronics, explore twisted bilayer friction, and engineer Janus TMD heterostructures for next-generation spin field-effect transistors.

03.A ‚Äî‚Äî
Nitride MXenes & Magnetism
DFT calculations predict tunable magnetism and spin transport in Mn‚ÇÇN and Cr‚ÇÇN MXenes ‚Äî our ACS Nano 2017 cover article established nitride MXenes as a new class of 2D magnetic materials. We study how surface terminations and electric fields control magnetic anisotropy and noncollinear spin structures.

03.B ‚Äî‚Äî
Janus TMD Spin-FETs
Janus transition-metal dichalcogenides break out-of-plane symmetry, enabling strong Rashba spin-orbit coupling. We design bilayer Janus TMD interfaces that optimise contact properties for spin field-effect transistors, quantifying spin injection efficiency and transport length using ab initio transport calculations.

MD simulations reveal that friction in twisted bilayer graphene operates simultaneously at two length scales ‚Äî atomic stick-slip at the lattice scale, and a larger modulation at the Moir√© supercell periodicity. This sub-Moir√© friction, uncovered in ACS Nano 2024, has direct implications for 2D MEMS devices.

We map the phase stability of M‚ÇÇX and M‚ÇÉX‚ÇÇ MXenes as a function of transition metal, coordination symmetry, and surface termination ‚Äî revealing design rules for synthesisable compositions. Soft Matter & Confined Water

Water confined in nanoscale geometries ‚Äî carbon nanotubes, Janus nanopores, biological channels ‚Äî behaves radically differently from bulk water. Using all-atom and polarisable MD, we study thermodynamics, single-file dynamics, phase transitions, and entropy-driven phenomena in these extreme environments.

04.A ‚Äî‚Äî
Water in Carbon Nanotubes
We established that entropy ‚Äî not energy ‚Äî drives water entry into hydrophobic CNTs. All-atom MD with polarisable water models reveals single-file water dynamics, dipole ordering under flow, and how nanotube chirality and diameter control thermodynamics of confinement.

04.B ‚Äî‚Äî
Phase Transitions in Nanopores
Monolayer water confined in Janus nanopores ‚Äî where opposite walls have different wettabilities ‚Äî undergoes a novel asymmetric phase transition not seen in symmetric pores. MD simulations map the full pressure‚Äìtemperature phase diagram and reveal entropy-enthalpy compensation at the nanoscale.
